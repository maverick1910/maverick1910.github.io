We all know that design thinking is called a future of innovating ideas, as it helps people to see a new opening to understand the problems and evaluate them. Although, people tend to have a misconception when we arrive at the word "design". Managers in companies often tend to feel uncomfortable when they came across the word "design" and "thinking".

When I first came across the concept of Design Thinking, there was a moment where i could not relate the concept to things that I had learned. It was indeed an eye-opener for me to understand a thing or two about it.

The Summary

Google says "Design thinking refers to the cognitive, strategic and practical processes by which design concepts are developed". But for me, Design thinking is merely about a problem that is either wicked or tame. Let me elaborate on it to you. Before we get to know what type of problems these are, let us understand that the normal approach that we use to solve problems in our daily day to day life is a linear analytic method, which is almost equal to a tame problem.

Tame Problem: With tame problems, we start with a good agreement about the definition of what the problem is. We often have fairly relevant data and we can determine cause and effect. And so we can successfully use linear processes and existing data to come up with an answer.

Wicked Problems: In wicked problems, the stakeholders involved can't even agree on a definition of what the problem is, much less agree on a solution. There may be a lot of data, but it's not clear whether any of it is relevant or how, and the situation is often sufficiently complex and fluid so that we can't really confidently predict cause and effect. And the only way to see if something works is to try it. This is where design thinking helps us as it is appropriate if a deep understanding of the actual people or users involved is essential.

In a nutshell, design thinking helps us in solving these wicked problems , if not solving them completely ...it helps us to arrive at a clearer view for a problem which in return helps us to form a solution-based approach.
